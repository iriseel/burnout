Is Your Pursuit of the "Good Life" through Repetitive White-Collar Labor Making Your Life Bad, Actually?

Or, an about page for this website

When I first started making this site, one text in particular weighed heavy on my mind: scholar Lauren Berlant's "Slow Death (Sovereignty, Obesity, Lateral Agency)." In this 2007 article, Berlant sketched out a phenomenon they termed "slow death" — the mass physical wearing out of working-class (as well as poc and aged) bodies by capitalism in spaces of production and in the rest of life.

"While death is usually deemed an event in contrast to life’s 'extensivity,'" in the domain of slow death "dying and the ordinary reproduction of life are coextensive." Owing to conditions of diet, labor, environment, and more, the lives of vulnerable populations are shortened at the same time as temporality speeds up under capitalism. "[M]orbidity, the embodiment towards death as a way of life, marks out slow death as what there is of the good life for the vast majority of American workers."

In comparison, burnout comes across as an almost benignly white-collar variety of slow death. Distinctions abound between immaterial and material labor, productivity and production, livelihood and life. Even as the intensifying conflation of work and home troubles the distinction, theoretically at least, burnout is meant to be a condition to be left at the workplace, as opposed to the all-permeating condition of slow death. But I nonetheless approached the subject of burnout with slow death on my mind, and in so doing found myself thinking most about (micro-)scales of time as a body incrementally frays against capitalist pace, physiologies of stress, the fine attunements our bodies make to become appendages of our respective machines, and the small repetitive-compulsive gestures and flatness of affect that make up various (literal) postures of capitalism in its different historical stages.

These concerns led me to a medley of multimedia materials, which are grouped thematically below.

Yet without any seeming criticism of the work demands that demobilized her, said intern Michelle instead pivoted to drawing on a trackpad with her nose: “I’ll still put in the same long hours, but I’ll just do it with my nose, and the nosepad, as I call it.” Her story is meant to be inspirational, somehow: “I want to tell other people that have the same problem and might think their jobs are in jeopardy that no, it doesn’t have to mean that.
